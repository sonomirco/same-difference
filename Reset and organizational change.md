Organizational change is hard because it's deeply human. The most successful change efforts start with "go and see the work," build culture before introducing changes, and recognize that middle managers are often the silent point of failure.

## Go and see the work

Nelson Repenning, an MIT professor, coined the phrase **"go and see the work"** as a method for finding leverage points to improve systems and processes.

### The concept

This approach encourages leaders to directly observe and experience the work being done, rather than relying solely on reports or secondhand information.

### What it involves

- Getting closer to the ground level of operations
- Directly observing how things are made or how processes unfold
- Gaining firsthand insights into inefficiencies or pain points

### Examples

- A school principal shadowing a student for a day
- A factory manager following production from start to finish
- A consultant mapping out the flow of activities

### The benefits

- Helps identify leverage points that are both doable and worth doing
- Allows leaders to discover ideas rather than just debating them
- Can reveal unexpected issues (e.g., factory owner discovering production inefficiency by walking the floor)

By going to see the work, leaders gain valuable insights not apparent from reports or meetings alone.

## Getting employee buy-in for organizational change

From [HBR: Getting Employee Buy-In for Organizational Change](https://hbr.org/2023/02/getting-employee-buy-in-for-organizational-change):

### The problem with platitudes

Most advice about building internal support reiterates perfunctory platitudes—communicate reasons, be excited. This results in employee cynicism, doubt, distrust, and negativity.

### Create culture of change acceptance

The most successful organizations create a culture of change acceptance far before they intend to introduce changes.

### Break change into micro-changes

When people have actual agency in shaping a change, they are significantly more likely to embrace it.

### The scale of change

- Half of American workers (50%) report being affected by organizational changes in the last year
- Workers experiencing change are more than twice as likely to report chronic work stress (55% vs 22%)
- More than four times as likely to report physical health symptoms at work (34% vs 8%)

## Change is hard—here's how to make it less painful

From [HBR: Change Is Hard](https://hbr.org/2022/04/change-is-hard-heres-how-to-make-it-less-painful) and [Product Therapy with Leah Tharin and Christian Idiodi](https://www.producttherapy.org/):

### What people want to know

When change is proposed, people immediately ask:
1. What does this change mean to me?
2. Why is it happening?
3. What will it look like when the change has been made?

### The initial mindset

People's initial mindset (based on thousands of years of change being seen as threat):
- **Difficult**: "I don't know how to do this, and/or other people will make it hard"
- **Costly**: "This will take things I value—time, money, identity, power, reputation, relationships"
- **Weird**: "This isn't the way we do things around here"

### Fear lives in the unknown

Humans can cope with hard work and loud deadlines. What unnerves us is the blank space between decision and outcome.

**WIIFM radio**: Every listener instantly tunes into "What's in it for me?" Titles, power, routines, even friendships feel suddenly at risk.

**Practical move**: Start every change story with a clear, personal "why." Explain competitive threat, missed opportunity, or customer pain in plain numbers or vivid anecdotes.

### The biggest anti-pattern: Delegating to "the change team"

Many firms create a Change Management office, then hand the whole job over. Real transformation unfolds in daily trade-offs and emotional bumps.

Only leaders who own the vision can supply the authority—and vulnerability—needed when unforeseen questions surface.

**Practical move**: Executives should keep the steering wheel. Enlist specialists for communication plans or workshops, but be present in Q&A sessions, model new behaviors, stay accountable.

### Middle managers: The silent point of failure

Front-line teams usually welcome empowerment. C-suites usually see strategic need. The sticking point is the layer in between—managers whose past promotions were earned by mastering the **old** system.

**Practical moves**:
- **Coaching, not just training** - Ongoing coaching lets managers see what "good" looks like, practice it, get feedback
- **Pilot teams** - Run small, well-supported experiments that show new model in action

### Big-bang vs incremental

- **Big-bang** for boundary events (acquisitions, legal restructures, system cut-overs)
- **Incremental, rapid cycles** for cultural shifts (product mindset, design habits)

**Practical guideline**: Use big-bang for events you cannot phase. Use incremental for behavior changes—each step becomes second nature before the next layers on.

### Measure outcomes, not activity

The only test of success is whether the new way of working improves **customer value and business results**.

**Practical move**: Define small set of outcome metrics—speed to release, error rate, customer retention, revenue per employee.

### Individuals can choose agency

1. **Assume goodwill** - Most corporate shifts are clumsy efforts to survive or excel
2. **Join the solution** - Volunteering for pilot teams, asking clarifying questions, offering to map risks

### Ultimate accelerant: Leaders who show their own learning curve

> "When executives admit 'I don't fully understand this yet—help me learn,' psychological safety rises."

Questions become normal, curiosity spreads, breaking the myth that managers must have every answer before acting.

## One-page cheat sheet

| Challenge | Helpful question |
|-----------|------------------|
| People feel threatened | "How will this change make your day-to-day work **easier** or your career **safer**?" |
| Middle layer stalls | "Which pilot success story can we showcase this month?" |
| Leaders vanish after announcement | "Which town-hall or Slack channel will the execs use weekly to field live questions?" |
| Measuring the wrong thing | "What customer or business metric will prove this new habit works?" |

**Bottom line**: Change fails when treated as IT rollout instead of human journey. Name the "why," keep leadership visibly involved, coach the middle layer, pilot before scaling, measure outcomes over activity.

## Willingness to pay: Love, Utility, Coercion

From [Jason Cohen: Willingness to Pay](https://longform.asmartbear.com/willingness-to-pay/):

### The tripartite taxonomy

- **Love**: Purchases driven by mission, reciprocity, community, or design excellence. Creates "allyship. Consumers are genuinely happy… they don't even consider the competition."
- **Utility**: Rational, feature-benefit trade-offs—cheapness, convenience, system-of-record trust. Yields "fair exchange of value" but not evangelism.
- **Coercion**: Contract lock-ins, monopolies, data captivity, predatory bundling. Outcome is "adversarial… customers want to leave; they wish some new competitor would arrive."

### For AEC

For sectors like AEC, where workflow tools often rely on data lock-in or enterprise contracts:
- Are we earning loyalty through transformative design (Love)?
- Through indispensable integrations (Utility)?
- Through proprietary file formats that make switching painful (Coercion)?

The aspiration: Migrate from Coercion to Utility, and ideally toward Love—by giving customers freedom rather than tethering them.

## Product sense interviews

From [Ben Erez: Product Sense Interview Framework](https://bobbypowers.com/review-reset/):

### Four-step routine

1. **Frame the user and the problem space** rather than leaping to features
2. **Probe for evidence**—past behavior, frequency, intensity—to size the pain
3. **Ideate multiple solutions** before committing, then converge
4. **Define success metrics and trade-offs** that anchor solution in reality

The goal is to **demonstrate thinking** rather than **perform memorization**.

## The Mom Test

From [Rob Fitzpatrick: The Mom Test](https://www.amazon.com/Mom-Test-managers-talk-about-ideas/dp/0985667318):

### Three deceptively simple rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea**
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future**
3. **Look for concrete commitment**—time, reputation, money—as proof of real pain

### The warning

Compliments and vague enthusiasm are lethal; they flatter founders into false confidence. The only safe data points are **facts about what people are already doing and what they have already spent.**

Discovery is an evidence hunt, not a focus-group wish list.

### Where they intersect

1. **Problem before solution** - Both refuse to brainstorm features until pain is proven
2. **Past behavior as gold standard** - People's past actions predict future behavior
3. **Bias-busting techniques** - Safeguards against designing for oneself
4. **Signals of commitment** - Skin in the game as ultimate validation

## Summary

Organizational change requires:

1. **Go and see the work** - Direct observation reveals what reports miss
2. **Create culture before change** - Build acceptance before introducing changes
3. **Name the unknowns** - Fear lives in the blank space between decision and outcome
4. **Keep leadership involved** - Don't delegate change to "the change team"
5. **Coach middle managers** - They're often the silent point of failure
6. **Match method to context** - Big-bang for events, incremental for culture
7. **Measure outcomes, not activity** - Customer value and business results
8. **Love over coercion** - Build genuine loyalty, not lock-in
9. **Evidence over enthusiasm** - Past behavior, not hypothetical agreement

The discomfort of change turns from wall into stepping-stone when treated as human journey, not IT rollout.
