Organisational change is hard because it's deeply human. The most successful change efforts start with "go and see the work" — directly observing how things are done rather than relying on reports or secondhand information. Nelson Repenning at MIT coined the phrase as a method for finding leverage points to improve systems and processes. A school principal shadowing a student for a day, a factory manager following production from start to finish, a consultant mapping the actual flow of activities: these surface unexpected issues that meetings and dashboards don't. Leaders who go and see discover ideas rather than debate them.

The research on change acceptance is sobering. Half of American workers report being affected by organisational change in the last year. Those experiencing change are more than twice as likely to report chronic work stress, and more than four times as likely to report physical health symptoms at work. Most advice about building internal support reiterates perfunctory platitudes: communicate the reasons, be excited. This results in cynicism.

The more durable approach is to create a culture of change acceptance well before introducing any specific change. People experiencing change immediately ask three questions: what does this mean to me, why is it happening, and what will it look like when it's done? When people have actual agency in shaping a change — when it's broken into micro-changes rather than announced in full — they are significantly more likely to embrace it.

**Middle managers are often the silent point of failure in change programmes.** They filter information both ways: what leadership thinks is being communicated often isn't, and what frontline teams need leadership to hear often doesn't reach them. Change programmes that skip the middle management layer, or treat it as a passive distribution channel, reliably stall there.
