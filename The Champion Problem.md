Construction has had an answer to technology adoption for years: put a champion in the room. Someone who leans forward in demos, installs tools on their own time, comes back with use cases. Give them a title — AI champion, digital lead — and the adoption problem feels handled.

Maryrose Lyons told the story on Bricks and Bytes. One company had twelve of them. They met every month, talked about new tools, spun up prototypes on Lovable, and clearly enjoyed it. But after a couple of years the business still worked exactly the way it had before — which is why they brought her in. That's not an unusual story, it's the pattern.

Those people are real, and that's the point. They're the ones trialling and learning and pushing the boundaries of how to use emerging tech to solve old problems. They believe in this, and that belief is valuable.

**But identifying a champion is not a strategy — it's the beginning of one.**

The firms that actually move give champions something harder: a small focused team instead of one person per department, two or three measurable use cases with clear accountability, and regular refreshes as the tools evolve.

Maryrose went back to a company she'd worked with years earlier and found them doing things exactly as she'd left them. The technology had moved on; they hadn't. The champion had simply maintained.

That's not a people problem, it's a structural one. A champion without direction, clear outcomes, or leadership that actively creates space for experimentation will eventually stop pushing — or become the person who runs the monthly meeting about tools they're not allowed to deploy.

The goal was never to find people willing to try — plenty of firms have those. The harder part is building the conditions that let them change something. Seen that way, the champion question starts to look less like who you appoint and more like what you're willing to restructure around them.
