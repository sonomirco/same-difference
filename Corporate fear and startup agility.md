Corporations are often tempted to look at startup successes and instruct their teams to "act more like a startup." This is usually a terrible mistake. A corporation trying to act like a startup is like a high school marching band trying to play improvisational jazz—it may sound like a fun idea, but the result is chaos.

## Corporate vs startup: different strengths

**Corporate strengths**:
- Coordination
- Efficiency of execution
- Resources and scale

**Startup strengths**:
- Nimbleness and speed
- Well-incentivized entrepreneurs
- Ability to reimagine and deploy quickly
- Tolerance for failure

As Jeff Bezos observed: **"Human nature is to overestimate risk and underestimate opportunity."**

## Innovation theater: The corporate equivalent of burning forests

Most large companies have piled up extraordinary "innovation debt." They engage in **innovation theater**—activities that look productive but don't create real value or learning.

This parallels the mismanagement of forest land and wildfires in the western United States:
- Everyone knows what needs to be done, but no one is able to do it
- Systemic optimization for near-term incentives creates problems that seem simultaneously easy and impossible to fix

**The solution**: Adopt a strategy of "controlled burns"—willingness to tolerate some inefficiency and bounded risk in the near term in exchange for long-term resilience.

> "Innovation is the result of learning, of systematic conversion of assumptions into knowledge, so it must be, by definition, capital inefficient in the near term."

In a corporation, a strategy of "controlled burns" looks like rapid experimentation, focused on learning and building future optionality.

## Process accountability vs outcome accountability

NASA's experience provides a powerful lesson. Teams routinely debriefed after training simulations and operational events, but what stood in the way was a **performance culture that held people accountable only for outcomes**.

### The problem with outcome-only accountability

- Each successful launch encouraged focus on results rather than faulty processes
- NASA was rewarding luck and repeating problematic practices
- Failing to rethink what qualified as acceptable risk
- Exclusively praising and rewarding results breeds overconfidence in poor strategies

### Process accountability expands the scorecard

**Learning cultures** consider processes as well as outcomes:

| Outcome | Process | Verdict |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Positive | Shallow | You were lucky |
| Positive | Deep | Success—better practice discovered |
| Negative | Shallow | Failure |
| Negative | Deep | Smart experiment—valuable learning |

### Amazon's six-page memo

One of the most effective steps toward process accountability is at Amazon, where important decisions aren't made based on PowerPoint presentations. They're informed by a six-page memo that lays out:
- The problem
- Different approaches considered in the past
- How the proposed solution serves the customer

At the start of the meeting, everyone reads the memo silently to avoid groupthink. The quality of the process can be evaluated based on rigor and creativity before results are known.

### Psychological safety + accountability

- **Safety without accountability**: People stay in comfort zone
- **Accountability without safety**: People stay silent in anxiety zone
- **Safety + accountability**: Learning zone—people feel free to experiment and challenge each other

## Disagree and commit: Requiring proof is the enemy of progress

In too many organizations, leaders look for guarantees that results will be favorable before testing or investing in something new.

> "Requiring proof is an enemy of progress. This is like telling Gutenberg you'd only bankroll a printing press once he had a long line of satisfied customers, or announcing to HIV researchers that you'd only fund clinical trials after treatments worked."

Amazon's principle of **disagree and commit**:
- Instead of demanding convincing results, experiments start with asking people to make bets
- "I know we disagree on this, but will you gamble with me on it?"
- The goal is to make rethinking so familiar that it becomes routine

## Why corporations struggle with experimentation

**Barriers in corporations**:
- Innovation is centralized
- Experiments are expensive whether they fail or succeed
- Over-communication leads to consensus that kills disruptive ideas
- Communication invites **vetocracy**—ideas killed by single naysayer
- Unanimity feels safer
- Incentives optimize for near-term results

**Contrast with startups**:
- Decentralized, small empowered teams
- Experiments are cheap
- Communication is minimal
- Failure is expected and tolerated
- Incentives reward upside, not just protect downside

## Corporate-startup collaboration: The path forward

Corporations must learn to collaborate with outside experts, small teams, and startups. The new, decentralized world order requires a faster pace.

### Successful examples

**Apple's acquisitions**:
- Siri → foundation of natural language and AI efforts
- PA Semi → "A" microprocessors powering billions of iPhones and iPads

### How to make it work

For incumbent firms, a more expansive reimagining of R&D, M&A, and corporate venture capital is required.

**The path to success**: Build and support fully autonomous startups that produce:
- Technology
- Talent
- Economic value
- Data
- Market lessons

Exploitable by the large partner.

### Innovation funding: Operating expense vs balance sheet

**Close to core business**:
- Fund as operating expense
- Impatient capital
- Compete with marketing spend
- Capital efficient
- Short time to payback
- Negative short-term drag on earnings acceptable

**Far from core business**:
- Fund from balance sheet
- Patient capital that can wait longer
- Challenges or looks very different from core

### Single piece of advice for corporate ventures

**Don't constrain a new venture's strategic or financial options.**

- Take a minority stake initially, with option to increase over time
- Goal is learning and optionality, not safety
- Incentivize world-class entrepreneurs to move quickly with autonomy
- **You usually can't be both rich and king**

Allowing a venture more freedom increases its chance of success—that's much better than being in total control of something that fails.

## Startups as a cheap form of experimentation

In the world of business, startups are a "cheap" form of experimentation—the equivalent of Amazon's individual organisms where mutation happens.

Innovation doesn't come from command-and-control. Corporate innovation is often pursued with the express goal of creating a new "superorganism"—a stronger and reimagined business model—rather than simply exploring what's possible. The objective is never reached.

**What works**:
- Experiment at the margins within constraints
- In pursuit of what is interesting
- Small, empowered teams
- Tolerance for failure
- Focus on learning over output

## The built world opportunity

About startups in construction:

- Many startups are getting money
- Construction space is attracting significant investment
- Robotics, automated processes, drones, data management
- LLMs and autonomous vehicles are part of the field already
- Going to explode even more

Big enterprises don't have startup agility, but having the mindset of partnership, collaborating, and nurturing valuable startups will bring them leverage and advance in the market.

## Prototyping in corporations vs startups

From [Prototyping is Often a Waste of Time](https://jmargaris.substack.com/p/prototyping-is-often-a-waste-of-time):

Corporations approach prototyping with:
- Many prototypes
- Many people involved
- Lots of planning
- Less action

Startups approach it differently:
- Quick, dirty tests
- Minimal resources
- Learn and iterate fast
- Action over planning

## Mirvac's Hatch program

An interesting example of corporate innovation in construction:

- Hatch is Mirvac's innovation program
- Creates internal startup-like teams
- Focuses on real problems with potential for impact
- Allows experimentation within constraints

## Summary

Corporations and startups have fundamentally different strengths. The path forward isn't for corporations to "act like startups"—it's to recognize what each does well and build collaboration models that leverage both.

**Key principles**:
1. **Process accountability**: Evaluate decisions by quality of thinking, not just outcomes
2. **Controlled burns**: Accept bounded risk for long-term resilience
3. **Disagree and commit**: Make bets without requiring proof
4. **Startup partnerships**: Use autonomy and minority stakes for learning
5. **Fund appropriately**: Operating expense for core-adjacent, balance sheet for far-from-core
6. **You can't be both rich and king**: Control vs success tradeoff

The opportunity for large firms is enormous, but only if corporate executives recognize the shift and take advantage of it.
