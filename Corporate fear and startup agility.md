Corporations are often tempted to instruct their teams to "act more like a startup." This is usually a mistake. A corporation trying to act like a startup is like a marching band trying to play improvisational jazz — it sounds exciting, but the result is chaos. Corporations and startups have genuinely different strengths: corporations offer coordination, efficiency, and resources; startups offer speed, nimbleness, and tolerance for failure.

Most large companies have accumulated substantial innovation debt. The activities that look like innovation — labs, sprints, hackathons — often produce no real learning or value. This is innovation theatre. The forest mismanagement analogy applies: everyone knows what needs to be done, but systemic incentives make it feel simultaneously easy and impossible to act on. The solution is controlled burns — deliberately tolerating bounded risk and inefficiency in the near term in exchange for long-term resilience.

Process accountability changes the conditions for learning. NASA's experience showed that an outcome-only culture breeds overconfidence in poor strategies. When each successful launch rewarded results, the faulty processes producing those results got repeated without scrutiny. Learning cultures score process as well as outcome: a positive result from a shallow process means you were lucky; a negative result from a deep, rigorous experiment means you generated valuable learning. Amazon's six-page memo externalises this — by writing out the problem, approaches considered, and how the solution serves the customer before a meeting starts, the quality of the process can be evaluated before results are known.

**The structural reason corporations struggle with experimentation is that innovation is centralised and experimentation is expensive.** Communication invites vetocracy: any single naysayer can kill a disruptive idea when consensus is required. Startups work differently — small empowered teams, cheap experiments, failure expected, incentives that reward upside rather than protect downside. The path for corporations isn't to become startups. It's to learn to collaborate with them, and to create internal conditions where controlled bets are possible.
