For a while I've been waiting to hear someone in construction describe how they actually changed their working day with an agent. Not the output — the day. How their Monday morning got rebuilt.

Instead the conversation is always about tools and what they produce: better models, faster drawings, cleaner renders.

Then OpenAI published a piece on how agents are changing work inside their own company. The engineers led, of course. But the part that stayed with me was what happened next: once Codex was integrated, the non-technical teams picked it up too — legal, finance, recruiting — until the average lawyer or recruiter there was running the majority of their work through it. The operational side didn't get left behind. It followed fast.

That's the bridge I keep coming back to between tech and construction.

Because we're still scanning the wrong horizon. The sharpest conversations in our industry right now are about whether frontier models can truly understand AEC data — drawings, IFC, specs, the structure underneath them. It's a good question. It's just aimed at the craft layer, the thing that photographs well in a client meeting.

Meanwhile the part of construction that could move today gets no attention. This industry is still made of people who coordinate: project managers, accountants, HR, finance. People who live in meetings, emails and status updates.

**The agentic shift tends to enter an industry through its operational layer, not its showcase output — and that's the one door we're not watching.**

Think about what's already possible from a desktop app. Record a meeting and let an agent pull out the actionable tasks. Run meetings against each other to surface the patterns and blockers nobody's tracking. Keep status updates maintained without chasing them. Feed that growing project knowledge back into an agent that drafts and organises your email.

None of this needs a better model. It needs us to look at a different layer.

What I'd like to see is the industry start talking about this — agentic desktop applications like Codex or Claude becoming the entry point for day-to-day work, the way OpenAI describes its own people already using them. Not the next tool. The next working day.
