The platformisation of AEC is reshaping where power and control sit in the industry. BIM shifted design and construction from 2D plans toward a data-first approach — design decisions validated on numerical ground, boardroom discussions anchored to economic policy and project viability rather than drawings.

Government-driven adoption created the diffusion: mandates promising reduced risk, faster delivery, and greater transparency across the supply chain. But the standardisation challenge has never been resolved cleanly. IFC — the attempt at a universal data exchange format — is closely intertwined with the technical development of BIM software itself, making it both a technical and a social process. The standard influences the tools; the tools influence the standard. Distinguishing the two has never been straightforward.

Cloud-API solutions are now making this more acute. Combined cloud and API approaches can bridge companies, processes, and data — but at the cost of making it harder to work in non-proprietary environments. Autodesk has turned Revit into the prime BIM-based data source, locked in most BIM users globally, and is becoming a meta-platform through cloud infrastructure, cross-subsidised by software revenues. **BIM becoming an obligatory passage point gives individual software companies significant power to define what construction data should look like, which data gets collected, and how it can be used and reused.**

Power has moved out of the relationship between designers and their tools and into the structure of proprietary platforms. The resistance — openBIM, BlenderBIM, Speckle, ifcOpenShell, Open Design Alliance — represents a genuine alternative, but remains feasible only for a small group of technically capable firms and researchers. Assessing the risks and benefits of digital transformation without accounting for this techno-economic logic gives a partial picture.
