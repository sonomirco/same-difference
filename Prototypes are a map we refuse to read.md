Construction runs on prototypes. Someone hits a wall on a project, builds a quick fix, and moves on to the next fire.

That instinct is about to get supercharged. When code costs almost nothing to produce, everyone becomes a builder of small solutions — a script here, an automation there, a GPT wrapped around a nuisance task. More fixes, faster than ever.

Tony Fadell drew the line I keep thinking about. He compared AI software to fast fashion: cheap, looks right on the first wear, doesn't survive the second washing. The luxury version is architected — it's meant to last. His point wasn't "don't build fast." It was the opposite: build *more* prototypes, because that's how you find the direction. Then architect the thing you actually commit to.

Construction does the first half beautifully and skips the second.

And this isn't a quiet suspicion anymore — the industry's own voices are saying it plainly. On a recent Bricks & Bytes episode, two of them landed in the same place. Rob Painter at Trimble: task by task we've gotten dramatically faster, machine control cuts earthmoving 30%, a robotic total station turns a two-person survey into one — yet the productivity line stays flat. His read: we made every player on the pitch quicker and never changed the formation. Ryan Maibach from the builder's side: his project managers now juggle around 40 software tools, and asked whether all of it lifted productivity, he said plainly, no. These aren't hunches. They're proof, on the record.

**Every prototype we never graduate into a real process is a fire we put out without asking why it keeps starting.**

And the proof is also a signal we keep refusing to read. The automations a team reaches for again and again are a map. They show you exactly where the process is broken — not the symptom someone patched, but the structural gap that keeps generating the same patch. Read the pattern of what people keep automating, and it tells you which workflow actually needs rethinking.

That's the move the industry hasn't made yet. We're still in extinguish-and-move-on mode, treating each fix as the end of the story instead of evidence. The voices are out there, clear and credited. The proof is sitting in plain sight. What's missing is the willingness to treat it as a sign worth a different conversation.
