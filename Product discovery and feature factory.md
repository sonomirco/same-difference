Product discovery reduces risk by doing research before building. The output is confidence — not solutions or designs. Yet many AEC teams remain in constant discovery mode without ever making decisions: all research, all documentation, all standardisation, no commitment.

The feature factory is the opposite failure mode. Teams focused on output volume rather than outcome value: success measured by features shipped, no clear connection to business results, decisions made by the highest-paid person's opinion. Both traps — endless discovery and mindless delivery — have the same root cause. Neither is grounded in a clearly stated hypothesis about what problem is being solved, for whom, and how success would be measured.

**The framing that breaks out of both: state the bet.** What we believe the problem is and who has it. What solution we'd build to address it. How we'd measure success if we were right. That's not a specification or a deliverable — it's a belief. Then identify the assumption that scares you most, design the cheapest experiment that could disprove it, run it, and change your beliefs based on what you find. Starting with the problem rather than the solution gives you roughly 2.5x better odds.

Discovery ends when you have confidence to make one decision: discard the idea, or build the first small increment. That's it. You're not trying to build confidence in the whole product — just enough to take the next step.

In AEC, the most valuable early move for digital teams is to interview the people who feel the delivery pain most directly, map the actual challenge, and start where the friction is real. Then limit work in progress. Will Larson is direct about this: by far the most valuable decision a team can make is to limit WIP, even if it means starting smaller than you want.
