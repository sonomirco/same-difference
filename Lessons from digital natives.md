Every company has to become a digital company. For AEC, this means finding the right balance between craft — the architectural and engineering knowledge that defines the work — and automation through computational design. The strategic question most firms aren't asking clearly enough is: what do you outsource to have the same baseline capability as everyone else, and what do you in-source to digitise something specific to your practice and develop competitive advantage? Many firms aren't in-sourcing enough of the latter.

McKinsey's four fights for digital strategy apply directly. Fight ignorance: use experiential techniques, not just training. Fight fear: build cultures where risk-taking is safe and the reasons for change are clearly communicated. Fight guesswork: base decisions on pilots and data, not intuition. Fight diffusion: focus on big moves that create real value rather than spreading resources thinly across disconnected initiatives.

NASA's Columbia disaster is a useful case for AEC. The organisational challenge wasn't technical — it was an unyielding hierarchy that failed to listen to dissenting voices. When organisations prioritise hierarchy over honest communication, the cost shows up at the worst possible moment. The lesson is structural, not personal: high-stakes environments require active mechanisms for surfacing disagreement.

**The Strangler Fig pattern offers a practical path for introducing digital change into legacy-heavy organisations.** Rather than replacing the old system all at once — which is high-risk and typically fails — you gradually route new functionality to a new system while keeping the old one running. The fig tree grows around the host; the host eventually hollows out. It's change management through architecture: prove value incrementally, let adoption pull the transformation forward rather than pushing it.
