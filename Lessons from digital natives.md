Digital companies have learned hard lessons about transformation, innovation, and organizational culture. AEC firms can adapt these insights rather than repeating the same mistakes.

## The 30,000 ft view

Every company has to become a digital company. This means:

1. **Finding the right balance** between craft (architecture) and automation (computational design)
2. **People undervalue** the competitive advantage that automation brings
3. **Automation balance** between off-the-shelf software/outsourcing vs in-sourcing:
   - Outsource to have the same base level capability as everyone else
   - In-source to digitize something specific to your practice and develop competitive advantage

**Key insight**: Some firms are not in-sourcing enough.

## McKinsey's four fights for digital strategy

From [McKinsey: Digital Strategy - The Four Fights You Have to Win](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/digital-strategy-the-four-fights-you-have-to-win):

### Fight ignorance
Educate teams about digital trends and technologies reshaping industries. Use experiential techniques like "go-and-sees" and war gaming to break old mindsets and assumptions.

### Fight fear
Address anxieties and resistance that digital transformation triggers. Build supportive culture encouraging risk-taking, learning, and collaboration. Provide clear communication about vision and goals.

### Fight guesswork
Base strategic decisions on data and evidence, not intuition. Use pilots and structured analysis to validate value propositions. Leverage real-time data and feedback to monitor progress.

### Fight diffusion
Focus on big moves that create value, not spreading resources thinly. Manage portfolio as dynamic system, not siloed collection. Balance digitizing core business with innovating new digital businesses.

## NASA's Columbia tragedy: Organizational dysfunction

From [Columbia Disaster](https://www.space.com/19436-columbia-disaster.html) and Amy Edmondson's analysis:

### The organizational challenge

NASA's organizational challenges contributed to the Columbia tragedy:
- Unyielding hierarchy
- Failing to listen to dissenting voices
- Management needs to step up

### The lesson

When organizations prioritize hierarchy over honest communication, disasters follow. This is especially critical in complex, high-stakes environments like AEC.

## Netflix culture in construction

From [Construction Genius Podcast](https://podcasts.apple.com/au/podcast/construction-genius/id1450374691?i=1000663791482):

Netflix's cultural principles have relevance for construction:

- **Freedom and responsibility** - Context not control
- **No brilliant jerks** - Cultural fit matters as much as skill
- **Open, honest communication** - Radical candor
- **Innovation through experimentation** - Test and learn

Can be applied to both tool changes and cultural changes in construction.

## The Strangler Fig pattern for gradual change

From [Martin Fowler: Strangler Fig Application](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/StranglerFigApplication.html):

A pattern for incremental migration:
- Gradually replace old system with new
- Each piece is migrated individually
- Old and new coexist during transition
- Reduces risk of "big bang" changes

Applicable to both technology and organizational changes.

## Business value over technology

From a conversation with Bill (data engineer):

> "I'm going to give them the same advice as if they were a bank robber. Go to where the money is."

> "Find business value. Don't get hung up on every technology that comes out. At the end of the day, business value drives everything we do in technology."

### The Willie Sutton principle

Why did Willie Sutton rob banks? "Because that's where the money is."

Why do you do with technology? You look for business value.

### The trap

It's easy to get lost and hung up on technology. Nothing wrong with loving technology, but:
- If you want success, find business value
- If you want to do fancy stuff that looks interesting, go ahead—but don't expect great reward

## Vision, Strategy, Objectives, and Roadmap

Many PMs struggle to explain the difference. From [Paweł Huryn](https://substack.com/@huryn/note/c-70834840):

- **Vision**: The future state you want to create
- **Strategy**: The approach to achieve the vision
- **Objectives**: Measurable outcomes that indicate progress
- **Product Strategy**: Defines product direction, positioning, and roadmap to achieve business outcomes
- **Product Management**: Guiding product success through customer understanding and team leadership
- **Product Discovery**: Practices for understanding user needs through research, interviews, and validation
- **Product Delivery**: Building, testing, deploying, and maintaining products

## The can-do mindset: Mindset, relationships, curiosity, simplicity

From [Stanton Chase: Digital Transformation in AEC](https://www.stantonchase.com/insights/blog/building-bytes-tackling-digital-transformation-challenges-in-the-aec-sector):

### Mindset

Projects pushing innovation boundaries require a can-do mindset: "there must be a way." This becomes the catalyst for pioneering ideas.

Innovation is not a destination but a journey—involving questioning the status quo and exploring new possibilities.

### Relationships

Building solid foundation with stakeholders and team members is paramount. Taking time to understand and connect with individuals lays groundwork for positive working relationships.

### Curiosity

Natural curiosity stimulates creative thinking, bringing innovative solutions not initially obvious. Encouraging teams to remain open-minded allows unconventional approaches.

### Simplicity

In a world cluttered with complex solutions, embracing simplicity is more than an objective—it should be a passion. Simplifying and streamlining are powerful strategies.

## Technology adoption timelines

**Microwave ovens**: Invented 1947, 1% of American homes by 1971, 90% by 1997 (50 years)

**ChatGPT**: Set record for fastest-growing consumer application ever—yet most people have never tried it

**The lesson**: Early adopters demonstrate success and inspire others to follow. Natural early adopters (like software engineers with AI) show what's possible—the more success they have, the more others seek similar tools.

Adoption won't happen all at once.

## Digital transformation business restructuring

From [Building Bytes: Digital Transformation in AEC](https://www.stantonchase.com/insights/blog/building-bytes-tackling-digital-transformation-challenges-in-the-aec-sector):

1. **Creating a Digital Business Unit** - Separate unit to drive transformation
2. **Agile Organizational Structure** - Cross-functional teams responding quickly to change
3. **Digital-First Approach** - Prioritizing digital channels in all aspects
4. **Integrating Digital into Core Business** - Embedding digital experts into existing teams
5. **Partnerships and Collaborations** - With tech companies or startups
6. **Investing in Digital Talent** - Restructuring HR to attract digital talent
7. **Customer-Centric Restructuring** - Around customer using digital tools

## Summary

Digital companies have learned hard lessons that AEC can adapt:

1. **Four fights** - Ignorance, fear, guesswork, diffusion must be overcome
2. **Columbia tragedy** - Hierarchy and silenced voices lead to disaster
3. **Netflix principles** - Freedom, responsibility, candor, experimentation
4. **Business value** - Go where the money is, not where the technology is
5. **Can-do mindset** - There must be a way; curiosity, relationships, simplicity
6. **Adoption timelines** - Change takes time; early adopters lead the way
7. **Product clarity** - Vision ≠ strategy ≠ objectives ≠ roadmap

The challenge: Technology literacy is rising, but we keep training people on methodologies/processes/software from the 1980s that were never updated.
