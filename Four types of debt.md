Product teams accumulate four types of debt when prioritising immediate demands over long-term health. Understanding the distinction helps make smarter trade-off decisions rather than treating all shortcuts the same.

Technical debt is the most familiar: shortcuts in code, architecture, or infrastructure that slow future development. Product debt accumulates when teams build features that don't align with strategy, add complexity without removing old options, or neglect product foundations while shipping new capabilities. Knowledge debt is gaps in understanding and documentation — tribal knowledge locked in individual heads, lessons from failures not shared across projects, institutional knowledge that walks out the door with every retirement. Data debt is poor data quality, inconsistent definitions across systems, missing governance, siloed data that can't be integrated.

**All four compound. Each shortcut makes the next decision harder, and teams become trapped in reactive mode — constantly paying interest but never paying principal.**

In AEC, the project-based model makes debt accumulation structurally worse. Every project starts from scratch. Lessons don't transfer. Custom solutions proliferate. There is no incentive to invest in long-term foundations because no project fee covers it. The fix requires deliberate choices: reusable components and standards, project learnings captured in accessible formats, internal platforms that scale across projects, investment in tools even when clients won't pay directly for them.

Debt isn't always bad. Sometimes taking it on deliberately is the right strategic call: speed to market over perfect architecture, responding to a customer emergency over writing documentation. The problem is unintentional or untracked debt. Making debt visible — maintaining a backlog, estimating the interest each item costs, scheduling repayment — is what separates firms that compound capability from firms that compound problems.
