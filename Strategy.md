Strategy is often treated as executive theater—alibi progress that looks strategic but guides no decisions. Real strategy empowers teams to say yes or no with confidence.

## Alibi progress: When strategy is decoration

> "In many companies, there's a tendency to take the CEO's ideas and repackage them in a Business Model Canvas, so they look like a 'proper strategy.' That's textbook Alibi Progress."

**Alibi Progress** = Busy work masquerading as strategic movement.

### Detecting alibi progress

From [Tim Herbig: Why Your Users Don't Care About Your Product Strategy](https://newsletter.herbig.co/posts/358):

> "One of the most powerful ways to spot and stop **Alibi Progress** is to start treating our practices like products."

Define for each practice:
- Audience
- Problem
- Success criteria

### Strategy isn't for customers

> "Strategy isn't primarily for customers—they care about your products, not your strategy."

The real audience is **internal**—product teams and stakeholders who need to make decisions.

### Measuring strategy success

> "Good strategy should be measured by:
- How much team energy goes to efforts connected to strategic priorities
- How frequently teams confidently decline opportunities that don't align"

**Reference**: [First Round Review: How to Make Your Product Idea Go Viral Inside Your Company](https://review.firstround.com/how-to-make-your-product-idea-go-viral-inside-your-company-lessons-from-figma-slides/)

> "Without a clear vision, every pivot feels like a step back."

Strategy must guide decisions—otherwise it's decoration.

## Strategy Blocks: An operator's framework

From [Chandra Janakiraman via Lenny's Newsletter: Strategy Blocks](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/strategy-blocks-an-operators-guide):

### The elevation gap

> "There is a steep drop in elevation between the mission/vision and the plan, and **strategy occupies this large void.**"

### Three to five strategic pillars

Strategy Blocks forces "a disciplined choice to deploy scarce resources for maximum impact," articulated through:
- **Three to five strategic pillars**
- Short list of explicit non-priorities

### Two horizons

- **Small "s"**: Two-year strategy in five phases (preparation, strategy sprint, design sprint, document writing, rollout)
- **Big "S"**: Three-to-ten-year strategy exploring aspirational futures via prototypes and user research

### Day 2: Scoring opportunity areas

The working group scores up to fifteen opportunity areas on:
- Impact
- Certainty
- Clarity of levers
- Uniqueness of levers

Top three become strategic pillars.

### Alignment baked in

> "Alignment within the team and with leadership is built into the process. The output is clearly defensible and transparent."

### Future-back thinking

The longer-range "Big S" track pushes teams to imagine "distinct futures" and build concept-car prototypes—mirroring the future-back technique Clayton Christensen championed.

## Great strategy starts with experimentation

From [HBR: Great Strategy Starts with Experimentation](https://hbr.org/podcast/2025/04/great-strategy-starts-with-experimentation) with Stefan Thomke:

### Strategic vision hardens into dogma

Strategic vision not continuously stress-tested through rigorous, low-cost trials quickly hardens into dogma—and dogma is lethal to competitive edge.

> "If you're not testing, you're guessing—and that's risky."

### The Bing anecdote

An individual contributor believed a minor tweak to ad placement might lift click-throughs; management shrugged. The engineer ran a controlled test. Result: **$100 million of additional revenue in that year alone.**

The differentiator: **"the power to run the experiment"** rather than the seniority to overrule it.

### It's not just for giants

Booking.com runs 30,000+ experiments a year (100+ per day). But smaller firms can still play—with thinner samples, test bigger changes to achieve statistical power.

### The cultural barrier

What thwarts progress is not tooling—but culture:
- Leaders struggle with humility of "I just don't know"
- Teams attack the tool when experiments contradict convictions
- One manager insisted software was wrong because fewer choices outperformed his intuition

### Experimentation as breathing

Thomke's prescription: treat experimentation as "breathing."

This requires:
- Guardrails (kill-switches, stoppage rules)
- Portfolio mindset (accept nine failures for every breakthrough)
- Behavioral rewiring (reward hypothesis generation, frontline autonomy)

### Experiments illuminate, don't dictate

> "Experiments do not dictate strategy, they illuminate trade-offs."

Leaders may override winning A/B results for longer-term positioning—but now decisions rest on transparent evidence rather than HiPPO (highest-paid person's opinion) lore.

> "Strategy is no longer a marble statue revealed after months of chiseling; it is a living sketch, revised with every new data point."

**Reference**: Stefan Thomke, *Experimentation Works*

## How to get better at strategy

From [Will Larson: Irrational Exuberance](https://www.irrational-exuberance.com/) - forthcoming engineering strategy book:

### From jungle myths to deliberate practice

You will not stumble out of the jungle rich in strategic insight. You must **design your own improvement loop** with four elements:

1. **Exploration** – Mine three resource veins:
   - Scarce public strategy documents
   - Richer private accounts shared through networks
   - Learning communities you convene yourself

2. **Diagnosis** – Apply consistent rubric to every strategy you study:
   - Slice into phases
   - Interrogate cost, longevity, failure modes, replicability
   - Train pattern recognition, don't collect heroic tales

3. **Policy** – Choose **one** concrete route to practice at a time:
   - Debug a failing strategy
   - Document an undocumented one
   - Improve adoption mechanisms
   - Launch new strategy at whatever altitude your authority permits

4. **Operations** – Track work like product team tracks features:
   - Maintain repository of strategies read, written, revised
   - Run quarterly reviews with peer or learning circle
   - When momentum stalls, debug with someone you trust

### Lowering the altitude

> "Only you can forbid yourself from developing personal strategies."

If your organization's ceiling feels low, drop altitude until you find space to maneuver—perhaps a small team or individual workflow.

### Time is not an excuse

> "Busy doing what?"

Rushing head-down often masks discomfort with ambiguity that strategic work demands.

### A benediction of fallibility

> "Even the best ideas here are wrong in interesting ways, and will be surpassed by better ones."

Strategy is less a fixed doctrine than a disciplined habit of curiosity, critique, and community.

## Summary

Real strategy requires:

1. **Detect alibi progress** - Treat practices like products: audience, problem, success criteria
2. **Measure by decisions** - Strategy success = team energy toward priorities + confident declinations
3. **Fill the elevation gap** - Bridge mission/vision and plan with strategic pillars
4. **Experiment as breathing** - Test strategy continuously, let data illuminate trade-offs
5. **Design your improvement loop** - Exploration, diagnosis, policy, operations
6. **Drop altitude** - Strategy at any level requires only permission you give yourself
7. **Embrace fallibility** - Best ideas are wrong in interesting ways

The connective tissue: **Make strategy visible, testable, and, above all, useful.**
