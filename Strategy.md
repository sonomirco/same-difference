Strategy is often treated as executive theatre. The tell is alibi progress: taking the CEO's ideas and repackaging them in a Business Model Canvas so they look like a proper strategy. The result looks strategic, guides no decisions, and gives everyone a clean alibi when results don't materialise.

Real strategy has a specific audience — internal — and a specific test: does it empower teams to say yes or no with confidence? Good strategy is measured by how much team energy goes to efforts connected to strategic priorities, and how frequently teams confidently decline opportunities that don't align. Without that, strategy is decoration.

The Strategy Blocks framework from Chandra Janakiraman is useful here. It forces a disciplined choice to deploy scarce resources for maximum impact, articulated through three to five strategic pillars and a short explicit list of non-priorities. The elevation gap between mission/vision and execution plan is where most strategies fail — strategy should occupy that void, not just describe the ends and leave the middle empty. Two horizons are worth running simultaneously: a two-year small-s strategy through defined phases, and a longer-range big-S track that pushes teams to imagine distinct futures through prototypes and user research rather than slide decks.

**The cultural barrier to good strategy is the same as the barrier to good experimentation: leaders struggle with the humility of "I just don't know."** When strategic vision isn't continuously stress-tested through rigorous, low-cost trials, it hardens into dogma. An individual contributor at Bing believed a minor tweak to ad placement might lift click-throughs; management shrugged. The engineer ran a controlled test. Result: $100 million of additional revenue that year. The differentiator was the power to run the experiment, not the seniority to overrule it. Booking.com runs 30,000+ experiments a year. Smaller firms can still play — run bigger changes to achieve statistical power with thinner samples. Treat experimentation as breathing.
