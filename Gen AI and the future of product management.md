Gen AI is fundamentally reshaping product development. Looking at a 50-year horizon (25 years back, 25 years forward), we can see a clear pattern: the internet solved distribution, and Gen AI is now solving delivery. This shifts the balance toward discovery and strategy, making product management harder yet more essential.

## The four historical problems of product creation

1. **Product Strategy**: Deciding what product to build and what problems it solves
2. **Product Discovery**: Determining how to solve those problems effectively
3. **Product Delivery**: Building, testing, and deploying the solution
4. **Distribution**: Getting the product into customers' hands

The internet made distribution nearly instantaneous (e.g., Netflix streaming vs. Blockbuster), shifting focus to the first three problems. Gen AI is now poised to disrupt delivery most significantly, with ripple effects on strategy and discovery.

## Where Gen AI has the biggest impact

### Product Delivery: The primary beneficiary

Gen AI slashes the time, effort, and resources needed for delivery:

- A team recently completed a **three-year tech debt replatforming effort in six weeks** using AI tools—an order-of-magnitude improvement
- AI automates coding, testing, and deployment, dramatically reducing the need for large engineering teams
- Delivery shifts from hand-coding to **orchestration and architecture**, led by skilled tech leads

Over the next 25 years, delivery could become as automated as distribution is today.

### Strategy and Discovery: Secondary but important

- AI aids in ideation and prototyping
- But the most immediate, transformative impact is on delivery

## The discovery vs. delivery flip

### The old balance

Historically, product teams spent most time on delivery:
- **5-10 engineers per product manager and designer**
- Discovery was critical but resource-light

### The new balance (3-5 years out)

As delivery becomes automated, teams could spend **90% of their time on discovery** and just 10% on delivery:

- **Reduced engineering needs**: Fewer engineers for hand-coding, but a skilled tech lead remains essential for architecting systems, orchestrating AI agents, and ensuring scalability and reliability
- **Discovery as the core focus**: Validating solutions, testing prototypes, ensuring value, usability, feasibility, and viability
- **Implications for teams**: PMs, designers, and tech leads focus on harder problems, requiring deeper collaboration and critical thinking

## Why product management becomes harder and more essential

Gen AI makes it easier to crank out features but harder to solve problems customers love and that are viable for the business.

### Value is harder to achieve

- Gen AI will fuel an explosion of startups and feature parity
- **Differentiation becomes key**—"we're AI-powered" won't suffice when everyone uses AI
- PMs must ensure solutions are substantially better than competitors'

### Viability is more complex

AI introduces new risks:

- **Legal**: Intellectual property, data privacy, regulatory compliance
- **Ethical**: Example—self-driving cars could save lives overall but cause accidents that feel preventable
- **Economic**: AI products are expensive to run (inference costs), challenging unit economics

### The PM's expanded responsibility

Product managers must ensure solutions are:

- **Valuable**: Customers want it
- **Usable**: Intuitive and trustworthy
- **Feasible**: Can be built reliably
- **Viable**: Legally, ethically, and financially sustainable

This requires sharper product sense and closer stakeholder collaboration (e.g., with legal or compliance teams).

## New must-have skills

### Tech Lead: The AI engineer

The future tech lead is effectively an AI engineer for AI-powered products:

- Expertise in AI models, data, and agent orchestration
- Similar to how mobile tech leads mastered iOS/Android
- Acts as the team's AI specialist

**Resources**:
- Tim O'Reilly's article [*The End of Programming as We Know It*](https://www.oreilly.com/radar/the-end-of-programming-as-we-know-it/)
- Chip Huyen's book [*AI Engineering*](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/ai-engineering/9781098101836/)

### Designer: Design sense becomes critical

Designers remain critical for their "design sense":

- Expertise in information architecture, interaction design, visual design, and user research
- Evaluate prototypes for usability, predictability, and trust
- AI-driven interfaces require new patterns (e.g., transparency in AI decisions)
- Tools like Figma's live-data prototyping empower designers, but value lies in **critical evaluation, not just prototype creation**

### Product Manager: Balance AI speed with rigor

- Master AI tools for ideation and prototyping without over-relying on them
- Balance AI's speed with rigorous problem-solving
- Avoid "feature bloat" and ensure strategic alignment

**Note**: There's been talk of adding a machine learning expert to the product triad (PM, designer, tech lead), but for most AI applications, the tech lead will absorb this role, consulting specialists as needed.

## Remote work: Can AI rebuild collaboration and trust?

### The challenge

Remote work poses a challenge for discovery, which relies on collaborative trust:

- Collocated teams historically fostered this trust
- The pandemic and global talent needs made remote/hybrid work the norm
- Current collaboration tools (video calls) are primitive and don't replicate the "healthy friction" of in-person discovery (e.g., Steve Jobs' "polishing a stone" analogy)

### The AI opportunity

Gen AI offers hope by enabling tools that mimic empathy and facilitate collaboration:

- Imagine PMs, designers, and tech leads working remotely but interacting with AI agents that tease out necessary friction (challenging assumptions, synthesizing feedback)
- These tools could bridge the trust gap, combining benefits of remote work (global talent) with collaboration quality of collocated teams
- Early tools show promise, potentially improving both product outcomes and quality of life by reducing isolation

## What excites and concerns me about AI's impact

### Excites

**Improved team topology**:
- Gen AI can reduce dependencies and tech debt by accelerating replatforming (legacy systems modernized faster)
- Smaller teams with broader scope (e.g., owning the entire checkout flow) gain more autonomy and end-to-end responsibility
- Boosts satisfaction and impact

**Enhanced problem-solving**:
- PMs, designers, and tech leads will tackle harder problems with greater autonomy
- Leading to better products and higher job satisfaction

### Concerns

**Job displacement**:
- Those who don't upskill risk irrelevance
- Especially in delivery-focused roles like product owners, which are tied to processes AI will disrupt

**Crappy products**:
- Using AI as a substitute for thinking (rather than an amplifier) could flood the market with low-value products
- Features become easier to build, increasing the noise

**Ethical and economic risks**:
- AI's legal, ethical, and cost implications (expensive inference) require careful navigation

## Direct advice for product leaders

### Start doing

**Adopt the product model urgently**:
- Move to empowered product teams focused on **outcomes** (solving problems) rather than **outputs** (features)
- Companies not embracing this model will be outpaced by competitors leveraging AI's speed
- Visit customers, share vision, coach teams on product and design sense

**Invest in strategy**:
- In a hyper-competitive world, prioritize identifying critical problems to solve
- Use AI to amplify strategic insights, not replace them

**Upskill teams**:
- Train PMs, designers, and tech leads to use AI tools effectively
- Hone critical thinking, design sense, and AI orchestration skills

### Stop doing

**Relying on feature-driven models**:
- Stop managing teams as staffing pools cranking out features
- AI makes feature parity easy, so focus on differentiation through value and viability

**Ignoring AI's risks**:
- Don't assume AI is a silver bullet
- Address legal, ethical, and economic challenges proactively to avoid costly missteps

## Summary

Gen AI is disrupting product delivery most significantly, automating building, testing, and deployment—much like the internet solved distribution. This shifts team focus to discovery (90% of effort) and strategy, making product management harder yet more essential due to increased competition and new risks (legal, ethical, economic).

Tech leads will evolve into AI engineers, designers will leverage design sense for trust and usability, and PMs must balance AI's speed with rigorous problem-solving. AI could enhance remote collaboration by fostering trust through empathetic tools, improving both outcomes and quality of life.

Product leaders must adopt the product model, prioritize strategy, and upskill teams to stay competitive.
