Looking at a fifty-year horizon — twenty-five years back, twenty-five years forward — the pattern is clear: the internet solved distribution, making it nearly instantaneous. Gen AI is now solving delivery. A team recently completed a three-year tech debt replatforming effort in six weeks using AI tools. Delivery is shifting from hand-coding to orchestration and architecture.

This creates a discovery-delivery flip. Historically, product teams spent most of their time on delivery — five to ten engineers per product manager and designer. As delivery becomes automated, teams could spend 90% of their time on discovery: validating solutions, testing prototypes, ensuring value, usability, feasibility, and viability. The engineering team doesn't disappear — a skilled tech lead remains essential for architecting systems, orchestrating AI agents, and ensuring scalability — but the ratio changes fundamentally.

**The reason product management becomes harder, not easier, is that Gen AI makes it easy to crank out features while making it harder to solve problems customers love and that are viable for the business.** Everyone will have AI, so differentiation through "we're AI-powered" becomes meaningless. Viability grows more complex: legal risks around intellectual property and data privacy, ethical risks in how AI-driven decisions affect people, and economic risks from the inference costs of running AI products at scale. PMs must ensure solutions are valuable, usable, feasible, and financially sustainable — a harder brief than before.

The new skill clusters follow from this. Tech leads become AI engineers: expertise in models, data, and agent orchestration. Designers need sharper design sense — evaluating prototypes for usability, predictability, and trust in AI-driven interfaces. PMs need to balance AI's speed with rigorous problem-solving and avoid using AI as a substitute for thinking rather than an amplifier. The concern isn't just job displacement. It's the flood of low-value products that follows when features are easy to build and nobody is asking whether they should be.
