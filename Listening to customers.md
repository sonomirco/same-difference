Most product builders misunderstand customer development. They're more interested in confirming their idea than confronting its flaws. Jason Cohen is direct about this: when you ask someone if they "would buy" your product, they are either lying or guessing. Either way, it's fiction.

Mistaking polite curiosity for commitment is vanity validation — a feel-good activity that shields you from the painful reality of invalidation. Real validation comes when someone gives up something valuable: time, money, credibility. Not when they say your idea is neat.

True customer development is about pain, not praise. The questions that matter aren't "would you use this?" but "how are you solving this problem today?", "what have you already tried and why hasn't it worked?", and "what happens if you do nothing?" If someone hasn't tried to MacGyver a spreadsheet or duct-tape a solution together, the pain probably isn't real enough to build for.

**Your ego is the biggest barrier.** You think you're listening, but you're hunting for confirmation. It isn't intentional — it's self-preservation. The emotional cost of admitting your idea isn't special is real, which is why founders either avoid the hard conversations or twist results into false positives. The solution is to build the muscle of invalidation: actively seek evidence that your idea is bad, and be ready to pivot fast.

In AEC and enterprise software with internal stakeholders, this gets trickier. Captive internal users don't constitute validation. The critical question is whether your users would fight to keep the solution — even if they had to choose it. Melissa Perri's build trap applies directly here: teams rush into solutions under stakeholder pressure without ever validating whether the problem is worth solving.
