Platform strategy in AEC combines product thinking with reusable service architecture. The practical question is whether to treat the platform as infrastructure — something other teams build on — or as a product in its own right, designed around the people who use it and iterated based on their feedback.

The long-tail model is relevant when many niche tools collectively create high value. Most engineering workflows have too many edge cases and discipline-specific variants for any single vendor to cover well. An internal platform that makes it easy for domain experts to build their own small tools — and for others to find and use them — can address the long tail that no off-the-shelf product ever reaches. This is the Micro SaaS logic applied internally: one product, one workflow problem, one team as the user.

**Product as a service improves adoption when users consume outcomes rather than raw tooling.** The IKEA effect works in reverse here: when teams have to assemble capabilities themselves from raw APIs, they often don't. When the platform presents pre-composed solutions they can use without setup, adoption goes up. Curation, discoverability, and feedback loops are therefore not nice-to-haves — they're what determines whether platform tools stay in use or quietly decay.

Internal composability — connecting web apps, shared data stores, and design authoring environments — is the enabling architecture. Standardised platform services reduce duplicate implementation across teams. Community contribution patterns and integration standards are what allow the platform to scale beyond what any central team could maintain alone.
