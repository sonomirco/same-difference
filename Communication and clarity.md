AEC's biggest barrier to progress isn't technology — it's communication. The industry remains locked in a fear of sharing, dominated by command-and-control culture, even as a generational shift changes how technology is perceived and expected. Reading *Paperless Builders* by Hamzah Shanbari reveals discouraging patterns: the same issues repeated at different scales, circling back through design, delivery, and construction. The core problem isn't what gets documented — it's that the why behind decisions, the reasoning for rethinking processes, the lessons from previous projects — stays locked in individuals' heads.

McKinsey's four fights framework for digital transformation maps the barriers directly. Fight ignorance: educate teams about what digital change actually means, using experiential techniques rather than generic training. Fight fear: address the anxieties that transformation triggers, build cultures where risk-taking and learning are safe. Fight guesswork: base decisions on data and pilots, not intuition. Fight diffusion: focus on big moves that create real value, not spreading resources thinly across disconnected initiatives.

The generational divide sharpens the communication challenge. Younger workers grew up with smartphones as an unremarkable baseline; they expect paperless workflows and streamlined processes. Veterans carry invaluable hands-on knowledge but learned through methods where extended administrative workdays were acceptable. Neither is wrong. Both are operating from their own rational baseline. **Reverse mentorship programmes — where younger employees learn from seasoned professionals while also demonstrating new tools — close the gap in both directions without requiring either group to abandon what they know.**

What the industry is still navigating is the early-majority crossing. Early adopters struggle to communicate correctly across the chasm because they're talking to people with a different relationship to risk, change, and proof. The message that works for the 15% who are already enthusiastic is almost never the message that works for the 50% who need to be convinced.
