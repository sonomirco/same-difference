The AEC industry's biggest barrier to progress isn't technology—it's communication. The industry remains locked in a fear of sharing, dominated by command-and-control culture, even as it undergoes a significant generational shift in how technology is perceived and utilized.

## The communication gap

Reading *Paperless Builders* by Hamzah Shanbari reveals discouraging patterns: the same issues repeated at different scales, circling back through the project's design, delivery, and construction lifespan.

**The core problem**: Communication about how things are developed—the **why** behind them—how we should rethink processes, learn from previous projects, and collect this experience (not just keep them stored in the memory of individuals) is where the AEC space struggles most.

Using "Crossing the Chasm" terminology, the industry still hasn't reached the **early majority** of users. Early adopters struggle to communicate correctly and aren't armed to be able to do it.

## McKinsey's four fights for digital transformation

McKinsey's "Digital Strategy: The Four Fights Companies Need to Win" underscores the barriers to successful digital transformation:

### Fight ignorance
- Educate teams about digital trends and technologies reshaping the industry
- Use experiential techniques like "go-and-sees" and war gaming to break old mindsets
- Deltek report: 54% of firms now prioritize staff education (up from 47% two years ago)

### Fight fear
- Address anxieties and resistance that digital transformation triggers
- Build supportive culture encouraging risk-taking, learning, and collaboration
- Provide clear communication about vision and goals

### Fight guesswork
- Base strategic decisions on data and evidence, not intuition
- Use pilots and structured analysis to validate value propositions
- Leverage real-time data and feedback to adjust course

### Fight diffusion
- Focus on big moves that create value, not spreading resources thinly
- Manage portfolio as dynamic system, not static siloed collection
- Balance digitizing core business with innovating new digital capabilities

## The generational divide

A significant generational shift is occurring in how technology is perceived and used:

### Younger workers (Gen Z and beyond)
- Grew up with smartphones as integral to daily life
- View mobile functionality not as innovation but as baseline requirement
- Expect paperless solutions and streamlined workflows
- Prioritize work-life balance (e.g., "making it home for dinner")

### Older generations (veterans)
- Possess invaluable hands-on learning and practical knowledge
- May need demonstrations of practical benefits (e.g., completing daily reports in field)
- Rely on traditional methods but aren't incapable of using technology
- Often extended workdays for administrative tasks

### Key contrasts

| Area | Younger workers | Older generations |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Technological adaptability** | Intuitively understand and expect digital solutions | Need practical benefits demonstrated |
| **Knowledge** | Fresh perspectives, strong technological intuition | Deep practical knowledge from years of experience |
| **Learning approaches** | Expect digital integration | Learned through traditional hands-on methods |
| **Workplace expectations** | Streamlined processes, mobile-first | Extended workdays for paperwork acceptable |

### Bridging the gap

**Reverse mentorship programs** can close the gap:
- Younger employees learn from seasoned pros
- Pairs 18-year-olds with 55-year-olds
- Mixes practical know-how with tech skills
- Everyone gains respect for each other

Companies should set ways for younger employees to learn from seasoned pros by pairing them up.

## Technical communication challenges

### The expertise trap

Technical professionals often lack skills to communicate value effectively. As Christian Espinosa highlights in *The Smartest Person in the Room*:

> The challenge isn't simply explaining what a technical solution does but clearly articulating **why it matters**.

Too often, technical advancements are:
- Dismissed as "fun experiments for geeks"
- Expressed using geek jargon that disconnects from the majority you aim to reach

### The expertise trap in high-intellect industries

From a recent conversation on technical leadership:

> "I think what you might call high-intellect industries, you see this a lot. So that would be places where a lot of the managers are scientists or dare I say even professors and other people that somehow think they know more than everybody else... You see it sometimes in IT, you see it in pharma and biotech, in science-based industries."

> "They really do believe that there's a right answer... Having the best product or the best technology doesn't necessarily win. **Customers have to care about that.** You have to be able to communicate that, convey that, build the story, create the ecosystem—all the kind of fundamental building blocks that great managers, great leaders understand."

### Product mindset for technical communication

Patric Hellerman's insight on contractor adoption:

> "If you come to a contractor with a robot promising whole new kinds of workflows, and leave all the risk with them, nobody's buying: you will end up in the valley of death. Contractors' willingness to take on untested tech is close to zero."

**Effective approach**:
- **Engage actively**: Understand non-technical colleagues' priorities and pain points
- **Simplify without oversimplifying**: Break down complex ideas into relatable benefits
- **Build trust through consistency**: Show alignment with organizational goals and measurable outcomes
- **Use demos and real examples**: Run simple pilot projects to establish needs

## Trust and blame culture

The industry has a trust problem:
- Clients don't trust contractors
- Contractors don't trust clients
- Much of this is consequence of contractual setups

### The project director pattern

A common pattern across projects:
1. Project director says: "We have no problems, everything's under control, we're going to deliver early and under budget, we don't need you."
2. Six months later: that person's been fired, project is over budget and late
3. Leadership says: "We fixed the problem. We fired the dude. We've got a new one. The new one says it's going to be on time."

### The blame problem

From an industry conversation:

> "I would love a world where information can flow up and down all the way to project directors **without blame**. Blame is one of the things that distorts this culture so much, including liquidated damages, which are a form of blame."

> "You don't get rewarded or you don't get credit when you prevent things from happening. You can unfortunately only show where someone didn't listen or didn't want to listen and things happened, even though you were warning."

### The human cost

This cultural distortion makes everybody unprofitable:
- Contractors/subcontractors getting paid late due to cash flow management
- People getting fired and unhappy
- Client organizations with huge losses on books

### A path forward

By removing perception of personal culpability in certain situations, transparency becomes easier. Transparency enables better collaboration and better outcomes.

## Adoption timelines and expectations

Change takes time. Consider the microwave example:
- **Invented**: 1947
- **1971**: Only 1% of American homes
- **1997**: Reached 90% adoption (50 years later)

In contrast, ChatGPT set a record for fastest-growing consumer application ever. And yet most people have never tried it.

**The lesson**: Early adopters demonstrate success and inspire others to follow. Natural early adopters (like software engineers with AI) show what's possible—the more success they have, the more others will seek out similar tools.

But adoption won't happen all at once.

## Technology literacy vs. outdated processes

Gradually technology literacy rises, but the industry keeps training people on methodologies/processes and software developed in the 1980s and never updated.

For the AEC industry, improving technology literacy means:
1. Moving away from outdated methodologies from the 1980s
2. Enhancing communication to help different teams and generations understand the "why" behind new solutions

Expertise alone isn't enough—it must be complemented by:
- Ability to build compelling narrative
- Creating ecosystem that facilitates collaboration

## Developing leaders effectively

A common practice: appointing the best individual contributors to be managers—without adequate leadership training.

**Better approach**:
- Know your talent well
- Make joint assessment: is there potential, interest, and aspiration to lead?
- Have those conversations early and continuously
- Create technical tracks: more senior engineers/scientists with bigger portfolios, not managing people
- For those transitioning to management: develop expertise in leading
- Most important about leadership expertise: constantly learn new things

## Changing composition of construction workforce

According to [Gerard de Valence](https://gerarddevalence.substack.com/p/the-changing-composition-of-construction):

The construction industry is **neither** a technological laggard **nor** an industry with unqualified, low-skilled workforce:
- In Australia: two-thirds have post-school qualification
- In US: over half have post-school qualification
- Share of unskilled laborers: ~10% in both countries

### The shift toward more qualified workers

**Australia (2000-2024)**:
- Professionals: 2% → 6%
- Managers: 9% → 12%
- Clerical/administrative: 8%
- Total in these occupations: 26%

**US (2014-2020)**:
- Nonproduction employees: 24% → 26%
- Workers with bachelor's degree or higher: ~14%

**Trend**: Toward fewer low-skilled jobs, more jobs requiring qualifications and technical skills.

**Why similar across countries?**
- Similar methods and processes in design, development, construction, project management
- Similar use of machinery and equipment
- Geographical dispersion of activity

## Conclusion

Most friction in the AEC space is about communication:

- **Friction between processes**: Every project has different teams, stakeholders—highly fragmented, difficult to define clear reusable communication protocols
- **Friction on adopting new technologies**: Lack of communication about why, sharing and opening discussion
- **Friction on learning from errors**: Lack of safe places
- **Friction on site**: Commander approach, empowerment unknown

Communication is becoming the big issue that isn't discussed enough in a space where:
- General commander approach is still used
- Empowering teams is unfamiliar
- It's better to cover errors than talk about them to learn and improve
- Fixed mindset is preferred over growth mindset

Without a fundamental change in how the industry addresses communication—especially in a highly fragmented and high-pressure environment—the cycle of inefficiency will continue.

The industry needs to:
1. Create safe spaces that foster constructive feedback
2. Celebrate good work
3. Learn from mistakes openly
4. Build compelling narratives about why new approaches matter
5. Develop ecosystems that facilitate collaboration

Technical people dominated by expertise will continue to have a tough time. They think their expertise, capability, and scientific skill set will win. But having the best product or technology doesn't guarantee success.

**You have to be able to communicate that, convey that, build the story, and create the ecosystem!**
