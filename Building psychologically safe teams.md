There is no ideal organisational structure. Centralised models offer governance and less technical debt but move slowly and lack domain depth. Decentralised models give teams full control but create duplication, inconsistency, and absence of standards. Federated and hub-and-spoke models can work well but require tightly integrated, aligned processes — a level of maturity most organisations don't have. The honest framing: each structure comes with a bag of sand you'll have to swallow every day. Starting centralised to build standards and best practices, then evolving toward hub-and-spoke as the model proves out, tends to work better than starting with the ideal end-state.

Ownership is one of the most powerful motivating forces available to a leader. It doesn't require large-scale reimagining of job descriptions. Allowing cross-functional teams flexibility and independence, letting people work on projects they can be proud of — these are low-cost moves with outsized effects on engagement and output quality.

**The feature factory pattern is the structural opposite of psychological safety.** Signs: success measured by features shipped, no time for discovery, decisions made by the highest-paid person's opinion, teams treated as delivery resources, innovation discouraged in favour of safe choices. The alternative isn't a management framework — it's an outcome: empower teams to focus on customer value and measure what actually changed.

Founder mode, as Paul Graham describes it, is the counterweight to over-delegation. Founders who stay deeply involved in operations — direct relationships throughout the organisation, willing to skip levels — get clarity rather than ambiguity. Brian Chesky: what everyone really wants is clarity. In exchange for some autonomy, they get a direction that's unambiguous and movement that's fast. The organisations that unlock collective genius are the ones where all important functions sit at the table — because the people without institutional knowledge are sometimes the most innovative, precisely because they don't know what's conventional.
