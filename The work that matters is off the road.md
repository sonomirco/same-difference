OpenAI is turning agent orchestration into something you download instead of something you build. You can spawn an agent for each task, let it do its job, and review the results. What felt like an edge a year ago is becoming plumbing: managing an entire fleet of agents is turning into a straightforward clone-and-fork task, a path the labs have already paved. But the real, important work happens beyond that paved path.

This raises an important question: if anyone can set up a fleet of agents, what truly remains to be owned?

In engineering, the written parts, such as codes, standards, and requirements, are usually the easiest. They are already documented. The real challenge lies in the last touch: the decisions an engineer makes after years of on-site experience, the reasons behind choosing this detail over another, and the judgments made when overriding specifications. None of this is written down; it’s not in training materials. It lives in the expertise of those who did the work and is faintly reflected in the drawings and reports they leave behind.

Therefore, the workflow isn't just the framework supporting the intelligence; it’s the intelligence itself. In our field, a generic agent assigned to a project might produce outcomes that seem correct but miss the critical judgments.

This means that the initial agent you set up isn't what gives you an edge. The real advantage is in the loop: the process of capturing why each decision was made across multiple projects until the agents stop guessing and start embodying the firm's actual reasoning.

What I find interesting is how differently this resonates depending on the organisation's size. In a large firm, operations resemble a federation spread across various regions, regulations, countries, and project teams that shuffle around with each new task. Its collective knowledge is dispersed everywhere, which often means it's not concentrated in any particular place. Conversely, small and medium-sized firms tend to operate more like a single, cohesive body. They can connect what they know more easily because there's less distance between the people who hold that knowledge. The real edge over competition isn't necessarily scale, but coherence. Big companies can get there too, but it has to start within a single team or department and then expand outward from there.

Either way, the process begins with a simple yet important step: deciding, for the project you're working on right now, to record the 'why'.

The valuable reasoning that can serve as a foundation is still in people's minds, waiting to be captured.
