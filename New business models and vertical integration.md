New business models in AEC are emerging at the intersection of software and hardware, open source, and vertical integration. Companies that own both the technology and the physical delivery have structural advantages that pure-software plays can't replicate — the physics, the feedback loop, and the margin all sit in different places.

In AEC, the trust barrier comes first. Engineers with twenty years of proven techniques have rational reasons to resist change — their approaches work. Trust must be established before tools can be introduced. Once people trust the team and the service, they're willing to try different approaches. The message is about the service, not the tools. The adoption curve adds another layer: computational design and digital teams tend to occupy the pre-chasm position — early adopters, roughly 15% of the curve — who get excited about technical solutions but struggle to bring colleagues along. Reaching the early majority requires a different kind of communication.

Open source is often misunderstood in AEC as giving away valuable IP. The more accurate frame is strategic. Making APIs available as open-source positions firms as leaders, attracts top talent, builds a culture of collaboration, and taps into broader expertise. Jeff Bezos's API mandate at Amazon was a strategic vision before it was a technical one — teams stopped relying on each other directly and built interfaces that could be consumed internally and externally, creating the conditions for rapid adaptation that Amazon's size would otherwise have prevented.

**The consulting industry's current breaking point is instructive for AEC firms making the same mistakes.** McKinsey's premium fees down 15%, EY's failed $700M transformation, Deloitte's partner profits shrinking while costs rise. The pattern mirrors what factory owners did with electricity in 1920: bolt the new technology onto old processes, expect magical productivity, blame the technology when nothing changes. Today's consulting giants are adding AI to broken processes, protecting legacy profit pools, and mistaking size for safety. The market isn't waiting.
