I attended a dinner last week where someone used an analogy that has stuck with me.

When cars first emerged, some towns prohibited them from their roads, citing safety and disruption concerns. The solution? Horses were used to pull motor vehicles through the streets. The rules remained intact, but the technology was forced to conform to outdated systems.

This conversation centred around AI, and that image kept resurfacing in my mind.

Today, most organisations aren't banning AI; the pressure to adopt it is immense. However, many are taking a structurally similar approach: layering new tools onto existing processes, workflows, and measurement systems designed for a completely different era. The technology arrives, but the underlying logic remains unchanged.

Jeff Witt, who manages vision AI deployments across a large manufacturing network, shares his insight from scaling across numerous sites: "It's more about the people and the process than it really is about the technology." The projects that stalled didn't fail because the models were ineffective; they faltered because they were treated merely as software projects rather than as operational transformations. True scalability was achieved when ownership shifted from IT to the plant teams closest to the work.

This pattern is also evident in AEC sector. The conversation often revolves around "which tool should we adopt or what we develop?" before there has been any discussion about what needs to change within the organisation to integrate it truly.

**AI adoption is not just a procurement decision; it is a transformational decision.**
