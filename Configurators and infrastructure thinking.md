Configurators represent a powerful approach to industrializing design and construction through rule-based systems, parametric databases, and standardized components. Combined with infrastructure thinking, they enable organizations to build platforms that scale rather than one-off solutions.

## What are configurators?

Configurators are rule-based design systems that generate solutions from predefined parameters, constraints, and component libraries. They capture tribal knowledge and unwritten rules that are impossible to document in a single unified place.

As explained in a recent discussion on construction technology:

> "There's too much tribal knowledge in construction that is impossible to capture in a single unified place... So we have developed our own technology in the cloud that performs this large-scale combinatorial rule-based approach—specifically to have the ability to get down to the detail of every part, every piece, every fitting, every bolt."

**The hybrid approach**: Combine precise, rule-based systems (exact spacing, tolerances, constraints) with AI's high-level pattern recognition and holistic understanding of building style, context, and construction methods.

## Modularization in construction

The construction industry has many modular elements that can be configured rather than custom-designed each time:

### Building components
- Plant rooms
- Roof systems
- MEP systems

### Civil infrastructure
- Bridges
- Tunnels
- Tunnel aeration systems
- Powerhouses
- Road systems

Reference: *How Big Things Are Done* by Bent Flyvbjerg—the real projects delivered on time are those that are modularized and repeatable (solar and wind farms).

## BIM-based parametric databases

Automated bridge modelling demonstrates the power of BIM-based parametric databases:

- Rule-based generation of bridge components
- Constraint-driven design that respects engineering requirements
- Automated updating across the model when parameters change
- Integration with fabrication and construction workflows

## Outcome-as-a-Service vs SaaS

A pervasive fallacy in construction tech is the "SaaS everything" approach. As Patric Hellermann from Foundamental explains:

> "If you look at the P&L of a construction company, typically 50% of costs are materials, 48% labor and machinery, and only 2% IT."

Yet many generalist VCs push startups to focus solely on software solutions, addressing only that tiny 2% slice of the market. This leads to products that fail to address the core needs of the industry.

**The shift**: From Software-as-a-Service to **Outcome-as-a-Service**

- Construction companies purchase outcomes, not just products or services
- Focus on delivering better, more efficient, cost-effective results
- Embed solutions in workflows and processes
- Don't promise "digital transformation"—back it up with results

> "If your tech can deliver the project better, faster, and more efficiently, show it by actually delivering. Don't promise a magical 'digital transformation'—back it up with results."

**Reference**: [Outcome as a Service (Foundamental)](https://www.foundamental.com/perspectives/outcome-as-a-service)

## Infrastructure thinking: Build platforms, not features

Toby Li from Shopify on infrastructure thinking:

> "How long would it take to build NFT gating?" "Two, three weeks."
> "Now, how long would it take to build a platform layer, which exposes APIs so anyone could build NFT gating in one hour?" "Two, three months." "Do that."

> "Because you don't know what they're going to build on top of the platform... NFT gating is one thing, one use case, but if you spend the time to build out the infrastructure layer—putting gas in the tank—people could drive on that gas for a long time going forward."

### The three buckets

When building something, categorize it as:

1. **Experiment**: Trying something to learn (may become feature or infra)
2. **Feature**: Taking advantage of existing infrastructure
3. **Infrastructure**: Enabling platform that makes many features possible

### Key insight

> "What can you build so anyone could build this in one hour?"

Build the infrastructure that makes the feature trivial—even if it takes 2-3 months instead of 2-3 weeks. After that, things built on top are so much easier, and many more scenarios emerge.

### Toby's development approach

- Write code against an API that doesn't exist yet
- Define the correct client code first
- Then implement the server code

This is infrastructure thinking: "What has to be true so that you could write this in a day? What infrastructure do you need?"

**Reference**: [How Shopify builds a high-intensity culture (Lenny's Newsletter)](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-shopify-builds-a-high-intensity-culture-farhan-thawar)

## Micro SaaS for internal tools

Every project is a big process, and every part can be improved with micro solutions. Taking inspiration from the software industry's **Micro SaaS** approach:

### What is Micro SaaS?

- Creates **one product** that solves **one need** for a specific market niche
- Operates in untapped markets with limited competition
- Bootstrapped or self-funded—doesn't require outside investment
- Can scale to thousands of users with big revenue
- Not concerned if growth stalls as long as users and profits continue

### Application to AEC

Create an internal marketplace of micro tools where:
- Engineers, super users, computational designers can access and use small tools
- Solutions target specific workflow pain points
- Tools are distributed and discoverable
- Successful internal solutions can evolve into external products

**Reference**: Spotify's Backstage as an example of internal developer platform

## Collaboration in industrialized construction

From Harvard Design Magazine on architects, builders, and the failed promise of deep collaboration:

### The original problem

The Construction Users Roundtable convened to answer: "Why are architecture and engineering working drawings so bad?"

They discovered the problem wasn't document quality—it was **lack of deep collaboration** between players in the process.

### Integrated Project Delivery (IPD)

The proposed solution—IPD—didn't work because:
- The building industry is highly resistant to change
- People don't trust each other
- Trust and willingness to innovate are in very low supply

### Two views of industrialized construction

**Pessimistic view**:
- Applying industrial process to design results in higher standardization
- Decrease in variation and choice
- "Design kits of parts"—decontextualized widgets
- You get what you get by fitting them together

**Optimistic view**:
- Better performing systems
- Can be overlaid on an agile design framework
- Design the best thing with contractors in the room
- All knives down, constraints loosened
- Set a standard that becomes a prototype impossible to ignore

### SHoP's approach

> "A foundational thesis of SHoP is that you start with design and convert design into industrialized processes. Design can be informed by means of production, and we manufacture bespoke 'capital-A' architecture."

**Reference**: [Architects, Builders, and the Failed Promise of Deep Collaboration (Harvard Design Magazine)](https://www.harvarddesignmagazine.org/articles/architects-builders-and-the-failed-promise-of-deep-collaboration/)

## The future: Industrialized construction is inevitable

The world is becoming more industrialized:
- Less patience for creative exploration
- Tighter timelines
- More demand for accountability of results
- Increasing automation
- People want results quickly

The UK, for example, is putting significant attention on off-site prefabrication of panelized systems and modular construction.

**Key insight**: The industry is becoming more industrialized. The question is whether it will be done well (with design leading the process) or poorly (with decontextualized widgets and kits of parts).

## Combining creative and deterministic approaches

The most effective configurator systems combine:

1. **Creative/design aspect**: Capturing unwritten rules, embedded knowledge from the field, things that never appear in construction drawings
2. **Deterministic/precise aspect**: Exact spacing, tolerances, constraints, purchasable parts and pieces

This hybrid approach enables:
- True design with actual, purchasable components
- Not just diagrams or recommendations
- High-level command and control with AI
- Rule-based precision at the detail level

## Summary

Configurators and infrastructure thinking offer a path to industrializing construction while maintaining design quality:

- **Configurators** capture tribal knowledge in rule-based systems
- **Modularization** breaks systems into reusable components
- **Standardization** creates consistency across projects
- **Infrastructure thinking** builds platforms that scale
- **Outcome-as-a-Service** focuses on results, not tools
- **Hybrid approaches** combine creative design with deterministic precision

The goal: Build infrastructure that makes designing and building faster, easier, and more scalable—enabling both industrial efficiency and design excellence.
