Everyone agrees: construction is inefficient.

Ask what the fix is, and you'll hear the same word every time: speed.

I was listening to Darko Todorovic of HTEC explain why enterprise AI pilots keep failing to scale, and it's the same story wearing a different badge. Companies don't adopt AI because they diagnosed why a process is slow. They adopt it because a competitor posted 20%-40% efficiency gains, and fear of missing out took over.

Nobody asked whether the process or the direction was right. They just wanted it faster.

Speed is the only lever we know how to pull on inefficiency. Quality and direction aren't treated as a fix; they're a nice-to-have, somewhere downstream of "fast."

Ed Catmull ran Pixar for decades on a mantra borrowed from John Lasseter: quality is the best business plan. Not speed, not growth rate. Catmull also refused to write a mission statement, because a mission statement is an answer and a company should always be asking the question instead: Are we doing the right thing? Are we going in the right direction? He never let that question close.

**Speed doesn't create an advantage. It multiplies whatever direction you're already pointed in.**

Anne Morriss, co-author of Move Fast and Fix Things, has a phrase for this: the metabolic pace of a team. Her point is that it is a leader's job to set pace on purpose and reset it the moment it no longer matches the direction.

Construction could borrow that. Check the pace against the direction. Reset it when it's wrong. Then go fast.
