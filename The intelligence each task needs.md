The industry is made of highly specialised disciplines, processes, and handoffs. Design, engineering, procurement, approvals, fabrication, and construction each have their own language, rules, data, and measures of success.

The current AI conversation is becoming louder every day, but one pattern is becoming difficult to ignore: the future will not be built by sending every task to the largest available model.

Recent outcomes-based comparisons have shown that technically impressive AI artifacts can still fail to produce the business result they were supposed to achieve. In some cases, smaller local models perform just as well when they receive the right context and operate within a clearly defined workflow. Under finite resources, systems perform better when they fit a target rather than trying to cover everything.

This does not mean that general models are irrelevant. They remain useful for ambiguity, interpretation, and communication. In construction, they may become the translation layer between fragmented disciplines: helping an engineer, contractor, client, and project manager understand the implications of one another’s work.

But the work itself will increasingly need to be divided into the right layers.

Structured data, rules, databases, APIs, or deterministic software should handle some tasks. They do not need a language model; there is little value in spending tokens to perform work that should be exact, repeatable, and cheap.

Other tasks require bounded judgment. These are the places where a smaller open model, trained or evaluated on proprietary data, can be more useful than a general model. It may know less about the world, but it can know much more about the specific task it has been given.

The important question for construction is therefore not, “Which is the most powerful model?” It is, “What exactly are we trying to do, what outcome defines success, and what data would allow a system to do it reliably?”

That is a more demanding question than buying access to the latest AI model. It requires understanding the work in detail. It requires identifying what information is currently lost between handoffs, what knowledge lives only in people’s experience, and what data needs to be captured consistently from now on.

**The advantage will come from knowing which intelligence each part of the work actually needs.**
