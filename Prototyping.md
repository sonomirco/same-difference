Prototyping is worth taking seriously as a thinking tool, not just a communication tool. The act of prototyping fleshes out product ideas far beyond what's possible in documents, spreadsheets, or presentations. It forces decisions that abstract descriptions can defer indefinitely.

The common advice — "just enough fidelity" — is less useful than it sounds. Fidelity has three dimensions: visual (how realistic it looks), behavioural (how realistic the interactions are), and data (whether the information shown is actual or representative). How much fidelity you need depends entirely on which risk you're testing. Just enough for a feasibility question is different from usability, which is different from value, which is different from testing viability with a specific stakeholder.

**The distinction that matters most is building to learn vs building to earn.** Discovery is about quick, cheap approximations. Delivery is about production-quality work that's reliable, scalable, and secure. Confusing the two is how teams end up spending significant time polishing prototypes they never actually validated.

A prototype can serve as a spec — it communicates the intended experience to engineers far better than a document. But that's the secondary benefit. Teams that focus on communication without first testing end up building based on assumptions rather than evidence. Tom Kelly at IDEO put it well: if a picture is worth a thousand words, a prototype is worth a thousand meetings. But only if you've actually run the test.
