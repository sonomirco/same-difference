AEC technology adoption is poor for structural reasons, not just cultural ones. The most persistent is misalignment between tools and how business success is actually measured. A digital team can deliver genuine efficiency gains and still be invisible to leadership if those gains don't connect to how the business measures winning. All stakeholders — including and especially business leaders — need to understand why a tactic is being adopted, not just that it's been mandated.

There's also a double chasm problem. Construction tech startups face the need to cross the adoption chasm not once but twice: first from early adopters to early majority, then again from early majority to late majority. Each crossing requires a different message, a different proof point, and a different kind of trust. Most tech adoption programmes are designed for the first chasm and stall at the second.

The centralisation instinct in AEC compounds this. Most firms default to centralised digital teams while simultaneously trying to deploy capability across decentralised project teams. The result: AEC teams work in silence, with no cross-communication and no common infrastructure for delivering or reusing solutions. Every project starts from scratch because the structure doesn't reward sharing.

**Spreadsheets are default not out of laziness but because off-the-shelf software hasn't met fundamental business needs.** SME contractors who actively customise spreadsheets are filling the gap commercial software vendors haven't bothered to close. Software developers have not pursued what lean thinking calls perfection — all the client's actual needs met. Until they do, the spreadsheet will stay.
