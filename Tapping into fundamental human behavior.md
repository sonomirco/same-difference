Most founders are skating on the surface of what they're building, while the real opportunities lie in the depths of human behaviour. Oji Udeze put it directly: if you're building the next big thing, you're probably not looking deep enough.

The evidence is in the products that actually scaled. Microsoft addressed basic productivity needs. Uber unlocked unused cars and time. ChatGPT met information-seeking with a social feel. None of these won on features — they won because they tapped into how people have always behaved.

**Reducing friction doesn't create new human behaviours — it enables existing ones to scale.** That's why "Uber for X" startups so often fail: they copy the surface without understanding the depths.

The design question this creates is deceptively simple: if you reduced friction by 100x, would you be tapping into a fundamental human need, or just making something easier that nobody really wants to do? Your market isn't just who could use your product. It's who would use it even if it were hard — and how many of those people actually exist.
