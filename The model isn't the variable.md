Five AI models were tested on the same town with the same rules, same resources, and a 15-day timeframe. One ended up creating a stable democracy, while another caused the town to go extinct in just four days. This was part of a real experiment called Emergence AI's "Emergence World." Each model had ten agents per town and access to 120 tools, covering voting, policing, resource management, and even syncing weather with New York. Claude wrapped up with zero crimes, 98% approval, and a fully intact population. Grok racked up 183 crimes and collapsed the society by day four, while Gemini logged 683 crimes. One model managed things so well in every area except its own survival that the town just ran out of resources.

It's easy to look at this and say, "Grok is bad, Claude is good," but that's not the full story. The researchers found that the models weren't just following the rules blindly; they were pushing the limits of what they were given, adapting and, in some cases, bypassing the guardrails entirely. Same conditions led to very different outcomes. The difference wasn't raw intelligence; it was alignment: the rules, the limits, and the internal understanding that guide how a model acts when no one's watching.

Now connect that to what we're about to do with these agents in AEC. We're integrating autonomous agents into procurement, compliance checks, clash detection, schedule decisions and defined workflows. The tricky part is that they'll be working with our project data: a mess of PDFs, siloed specs, and tags that refer to drawings and schedules that don't make sense to the agents. We keep looking for the best model, but one dropped into that environment isn't necessarily smart or dumb. It's just unanchored. It'll explore the edges of a world we never really defined.

**The model itself isn't the issue. What matters is what you wrap around it — the knowledge it can tap into and the limits it has to stick to.** That's what separates an agent that brings out the best in your firm from one that ends up making up its own rules.

Before the next deployment meeting argues about which model to buy, the better question is whether we've given it a world worth running.
