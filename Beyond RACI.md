RACI is often requested to solve collaboration problems, but it typically signals deeper dysfunction. High-performing teams don't need rigid frameworks—strong collaboration makes them redundant.

## RACI isn't a cure-all

From [These Are Systems: Beyond RACI](https://thesearesystems.substack.com/p/task-estimation-conquering-hofstadters) and [The Product Experience Podcast](https://podcasts.apple.com/au/podcast/the-product-experience/id1447100407?l=en-GB&i=1000720843104):

### What teams actually need

When teams request a RACI chart, they're rarely asking for what they need. The framework attempts to solve collaboration problems by documenting roles, but masks three fundamental issues:

**1. Team structure challenges**
- Essential skills missing from the team
- Communication flows inefficiently through hierarchies rather than directly
- Clear documentation doesn't help when leaders work toward different visions

**2. Mission clarity issues**
- Lack of shared understanding of purpose or strategic direction
- Uncertainty about what work needs doing leads to confusion about who should do it
- Strategic ambiguity can't be resolved by assigning roles—requires alignment on goals

**3. Handoff mentality problems**
- Factory-era thinking: work passes linearly from person to person
- Conflicts with collaborative, iterative nature of modern product development
- Best outcomes emerge from continuous partnership, not sequential handoffs

### The real solution

These underlying problems require sustained leadership attention and cultural change. Simply documenting dysfunction entrenches it by providing false sense that issues have been addressed.

## Healthy teams don't need RACI

High-performing teams never request RACI. When collaboration functions effectively, team members naturally understand their contributions and adapt fluidly.

### The Spotify example

A QA team member prevented poor product decision by pointing out a proposed design solution merely pushed user confusion to the next screen. In a RACI environment, this input might never have emerged—QA typically isn't "responsible" for product design.

The intervention was possible because the team shared a common mission and felt empowered to contribute wherever expertise could add value.

### "Everyone's a bit of everything"

While individuals have areas of specialization, healthy teams serve as designers, product managers, engineers, and quality advocates when the situation demands.

**Shared accountability over individual responsibility**:
- Team members focus on collective success
- When releases are at risk, PMs contribute to manual testing
- When UX problems emerge, engineers participate in design discussions

### Signs of team health

- Absence of territorial behavior
- No blame-shifting
- Energy spent solving problems, not defining boundaries
- Expertise as shared resource, not individual property

## Use RACI as scaffolding, not permanent infrastructure

When RACI proves useful, it should function as **temporary scaffolding**—facilitating conversations, then removed once real foundation (trust and understanding) is established.

### The right context for RACI

- **Time-boxed, scope-limited projects** involving people who don't frequently work together
- Structured conversation about roles can accelerate initial collaboration
- **Alternative frameworks often prove more effective**

### Implementation as scaffolding

Use RACI to surface confusion and facilitate relationship-building conversations, then **explicitly discard** once understanding emerges.

One successful approach:
1. Create RACI matrix before offsite
2. Discuss insights it revealed
3. **Physically throw it away** at the meeting
4. Establish more flexible working agreement

### Scaffolding vs infrastructure

- **Scaffolding**: Temporary support, removed when no longer needed
- **Infrastructure**: Permanent, requires ongoing maintenance

RACI should never become infrastructure—it creates rigidity that inhibits adaptive collaboration.

### Avoiding the enshrinement trap

Make clear that RACI is:
- Starting point for conversation, not binding contract
- Commit to regular revisiting and modification based on evolving understanding

## Language matters: Clarity beats clever acronyms

RACI's confusing terminology undermines its purpose.

### Responsible vs Accountable confusion

These terms sound synonymous in everyday language, yet RACI assigns them different meanings requiring memorization.

### The Consulted problem

Everyone wants to be consulted, but few want to commit the time meaningful consultation requires. Result:
- Delayed decisions due to excessive consultation
- Superficial input adding little value while creating illusion of inclusion

### Better terminology options

Focus on **decision-making** rather than general responsibilities:
- **Decide**
- **Recommend**
- **Advise**

These terms more clearly delineate roles in specific choices, avoiding linguistic ambiguity.

**Lesson**: Clarity of language directly impacts effectiveness. When team members must constantly reference definitions to understand roles, the framework creates complexity rather than reducing it.

## Better alternatives: Modern frameworks for modern work

### RAPID Framework

(Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide) offers superior clarity by explicitly focusing on decision-making processes.

Each role relates directly to how decisions get made, eliminating confusion about abstract concepts like "accountability."

### DACI Model

(Decide, Approve, Consult, Inform) provides clearer guidance on decision flow.

### The lesson

Better frameworks exist that avoid RACI's pitfalls while providing clearer guidance for teams.

## Summary

- **RACI signals dysfunction** - Team structure, mission clarity, or handoff mentality issues
- **Healthy teams don't need it** - Strong collaboration makes frameworks redundant
- **Shared accountability** - Collective success over individual domains
- **Use as scaffolding** - Temporary support, not permanent infrastructure
- **Language matters** - Clarity beats clever acronyms
- **Better alternatives** - RAPID, DACI focus on decision-making, not role definitions

The best teams solve problems together, not by defining boundaries.
