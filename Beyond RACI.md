When teams request a RACI chart, they're rarely asking for what they actually need. RACI is requested to solve collaboration problems, but it typically signals deeper dysfunction rather than resolving it. The framework attempts to fix three underlying issues by documenting roles — and it misses all three.

The first is team structure: essential skills are missing, communication flows through hierarchies rather than directly, or leaders are working toward different visions. The second is mission clarity: when there's no shared understanding of purpose or strategic direction, uncertainty about what work needs doing creates confusion about who should do it — and assigning roles to the confusion doesn't resolve it. The third is handoff mentality: factory-era thinking that treats work as passing linearly from person to person, which conflicts with the iterative, collaborative nature of actual product development.

High-performing teams never request RACI. When collaboration works, team members naturally understand their contributions and adapt fluidly. A QA team member at Spotify prevented a poor product decision by pointing out that a proposed design merely pushed user confusion to the next screen. In a RACI environment, that input would never have emerged — QA isn't "responsible" for product design. It was possible because the team shared a mission and felt empowered to contribute wherever expertise could add value.

**RACI should function as scaffolding, not infrastructure.** Use it to surface confusion and facilitate conversations about roles, then explicitly discard it once understanding emerges. One approach: create the matrix before an offsite, discuss what it revealed, then physically throw it away and establish a more flexible working agreement. The moment RACI becomes a permanent document, it creates rigidity that inhibits exactly the adaptive collaboration it was meant to enable.

The terminology doesn't help. Responsible and Accountable sound synonymous in everyday language; RACI assigns them different meanings that require memorisation. Consulted sounds inclusive; in practice it delays decisions while adding the illusion of participation. Frameworks that focus on decision-making rather than general responsibilities tend to work better.
