I keep coming back to something Brian Chesky said about industrial design: there are no product managers, because the designer *is* the product manager. You don't just decide how something looks, you own the question of whether anyone actually needs it. A design that nobody buys isn't a near miss, it's a failure.

That stuck with me, because almost everything we make in construction is a product too. The building, of course, but also the model, the design workflow, the spec we hand to the next discipline. Each one is something we make for someone else to use. The difference is that our work is fragmented by discipline, so everyone owns their slice of the execution, and somehow nobody ends up owning the question of whether the thing should exist in that form at all.

I think that gap is about to matter a lot more, because of where AI is actually landing. Most firms are pointing it at the slice: automate the documentation, speed up the compliance check, halve the modelling time. All of that is real and useful, and I'm not dismissing it.

But it's worth noticing what Andrej Karpathy said recently when he called vibe coding already obsolete. What he described instead — writing the spec, supervising the plan, judging whether the output is actually right — is, as Jeff Gothelf pointed out, just product management wearing engineering vocabulary. Once execution stops being the bottleneck, the bottleneck quietly moves to deciding what is worth building in the first place.

That's the part I find genuinely interesting for our industry. We've spent a lot of energy speeding up the boring thing and treated that as the win. But the boring thing was never the hard part. The hard part was always the judgment we kept deferring, because we were too busy producing to stop and ask the better question. **Automation doesn't hand that time back to us; it quietly promotes the whole industry into the product manager's job — deciding what's worth building — whether we asked for it or not.**

Which reframes the risk. For a long time the danger in AEC was moving too slowly. Increasingly the danger is building the wrong thing faster and more confidently than before, and calling it progress because the dashboard says we were productive.

So the question I'd be sitting with, if I were deciding where AI goes in a firm, isn't how many tasks we can automate. It's a quieter one: as the work speeds up, who is actually responsible for deciding what's worth building at all — whether this model, this report, this workflow even needs to exist in the form we keep producing it. That role barely exists in AEC today, and it might be the one worth creating before we automate everything around it.
